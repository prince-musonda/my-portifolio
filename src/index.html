Amora Store

I have been working on a full-stack e-commerce web application lately. My younger brother, Melvin, is deeply interested in entrepreneurship. He aspires to own his own company one day and establish a venture capital platform similar to Y Combinator, but with a specific focus on providing not only capital but also mentorship and additional opportunities to African entrepreneurs, startups, and innovators. Several months ago, Melvin approached me to build an e-commerce web app for him before he graduated from high school. Despite being occupied with work, I agreed to take on the project. Unfortunately, around that time, I was selected into the Education USA Scholars program by the USA Embassy. This commitment required me to begin preparing for the SAT exams, leaving me with only seven weeks to get ready. After writing the exams in late August, I shifted my focus to applying to colleges, a process that lasted until early January this year. During this period, I had little to no time to work on the e-commerce app. However, after completing my college applications, I finally began working on the e-commerce web app. I've been building it using Next.js 14, MongoDB, Express.js, and Node.js. I was unfamiliar with Next.js when I began building this web application. However, I decided to learn it as I progressed with the project. What better way to gain familiarity than through hands-on experience? This hands-on approach seemed like an effective way to learn. Originally, I planned to use bare React.js, but given that this was a production-ready application, I needed to consider aspects such as server-side rendering for search engine optimization, routing, and image optimization. This is where Next.js truly excelled. For the web app itself, I've developed a separate a Admin Dashboard and implemented authentication and authorization on both the client and server sides. It's important to note that I am continually making changes and adding more features to enhance the overall functionality.
